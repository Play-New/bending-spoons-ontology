# The market of targets — the sellers

**Properties** (per `../../ontology.md` §1 `Target`; backing = `targets.csv`): the nine gates — revenue scale ($50M–$5B) · HQ (Europe / N. America) · product offering (digital: consumer or enterprise) · revenue model (self-serve / sales-led subs / advertising; not IT-services) · room-for-improvement · predictable earnings (retention) · owner willingness-to-sell · valuation level · AI-pressure on core — plus the pipeline-state written by the actions: status (set by `screen`) · irr {levered, unlevered} · walk-away price (set by `underwrite`; per-target values undisclosed in the filings → n.d. in the backing).
**Links:** none declared on `Target` — `Deal —of→ Target` is declared on the `Deal` (declared once).

The second customer world model, and the one most companies would miss: for an acquisition machine, a "customer" is also **the owner who sells**. The deal business serves the market of buyable targets, and the recurring value is discovering and understanding that market before anyone else (see `../../capabilities/actions/market-radar.md`).

## The target profile
The thing Bending Spoons hunts is a **good-but-broken digital business**: a digital-first product with a loyal, sticky base but stalled growth, bloated costs, or a distracted/motivated owner. The F-1's own screening factors, applied by manual analysis to find attractive targets: **revenue scale, headquarters location, product offering, and revenue model** (`bsp-f1` ~L448). The four characteristics they select on (`bsp-f1` ~L2579-2589): **digital** (consumer or enterprise); **significant room for improvement** (can benefit from the Platform + AI to raise revenue or cut costs); **large revenue base** (transformation effort does not scale linearly with revenue, so bigger, less-optimized businesses are preferred); **predictable earnings** (strong retention). They favor returns over organic growth and are "largely neutral" on a target's organic trend (`bsp-f1` ~L162-164, ~L2591). The hard criteria on the identified universe were estimated annual revenue between $50M and $5B and a headquarters in Europe or North America (`bsp-f1` ~L449/~L452). Deal types seen in the portfolio: full equity acquisition (Eventbrite — 100% of equity for $505M; `bsp-f1` ~L4400), carve-out (AOL, from its parent — the combined financials are prepared "on a carve-out basis," `bsp-f1` ~L218), and asset deal (Mosaic, acquired 2024, `bsp-f1` ~L1863). `[to-validate — press only]` The "take-private" framing of the formerly public targets (Vimeo, Eventbrite, Brightcove) is a press characterization; the filings record the acquisitions but do not use "take-private."

## The funnel (the sellers as a pipeline)
The market is worked as a funnel, not waited on — and the 2025 funnel is quantified: over 2,500 opportunities sourced (most below the preferred revenue range), more than 200 analyzed in depth, 6 closed (`bsp-f1` ~L2617). Cadence of closes: 1 (2023), 5 (2024), 6 (2025), 2 (Q1 2026); more than 50 acquisitions since founding in 2013, 2017 the only year with more than ten, and two years with none — most recent 2020, when resources went to a pro bono COVID contact-tracing app (`bsp-f1` ~L327-330). The first deal ever cost $10,000, against ~$40,000 of seed capital (`bsp-f1` ~L170). Recent closes and disclosed considerations: **AOL $1,454M** (Jan 2, 2026; `bsp-f1` ~L216, price ~L3114), **Vimeo $1,359M** (acquired Nov 24, 2025; `bsp-f1` ~L213, price ~L3114), **Eventbrite $505M** (Mar 10/11, 2026; `bsp-f1` ~L4400), and **Tractive $781M** (plus $119M deferred consideration payable one year after closing; closed May 18, 2026, excluded from the filing's operating stats; `bsp-f1` ~L4599, ~L231; `bsp-424b4` ~L4702).

## The buy-side posture
Underwrite to the IRR hurdle — 65% on a levered basis and 25% on an unlevered basis for acquisitions completed 2023 through Q1 2026, held stable even as deployed capital rose from $194M in 2023 to $2.01B in Q1 2026 (`bsp-f1` ~L110-111/~L334) — and to a *long hold*: "We intend to hold and operate the businesses we acquire indefinitely, with the objective of generating and reinvesting earnings to deliver strong long-term shareholder returns" (`bsp-f1` ~L2595). That indefinite hold lets them justify paying up to the maximum their own five-year-free-cash-flow model supports rather than the minimum (see `../../will.md`). `[to-validate — press only]` The "prefer bilateral deals over auctions" posture is not stated in the filings — the F-1 only notes competition from sponsors and strategics can raise purchase prices (`bsp-f1` ~L555/~L724).

## The addressable market of sellers (sized)
The filing sizes the queue: more than 1,000 digital businesses (private and public) identified as potentially attractive targets, representing nearly $400B in aggregate estimated annual revenue in 2025 (`bsp-f1` ~L177/~L318). The identification started from tens of thousands of private and thousands of public businesses (`bsp-f1` ~L446). By revenue band among the >1,000 identified: 347 at $50–100M, 476 at $100–500M, 114 at $500M–1B, and 94 at $1–5B (`bsp-f1` ~L450); of them 791 are in North America, generating $315B in aggregate estimated annual revenue (`bsp-f1` ~L454). For context on where existing revenue sits today: 65% of revenue was generated in North America, 21% in Europe, 6% in Asia, 3% in Central and South America, 5% rest of world (`bsp-f1` ~L428).

## The thesis under it
The strategic reason the pipeline regenerates itself is, in fact, stated in the filing: "as many companies lack diversification and may not be well equipped to leverage AI, certain owners' willingness to sell could increase, contributing to lower valuation levels and more attractive acquisition targets" (`bsp-f1` ~L312). So AI pressure on incumbents' cores refilling the queue at compressed valuations is a filing-grounded driver, not just founder commentary. What the F-1 *also* supports is the discipline (IRR hurdles, indefinite hold, manual sourcing on the four factors) and the cadence.
