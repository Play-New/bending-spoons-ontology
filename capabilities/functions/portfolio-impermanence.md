# The portfolio is impermanent by design

The single fact that kills the mirror model: the businesses generating **100% of revenue in Q1 2024 had declined to 24% of revenue by Q1 2026** as new acquisitions were completed (`bsp-f1`, ~L401). The portfolio is not the company; it is a moving target the machine keeps refreshing.

```
function: impermanence              # ontology.md §2; the portfolio-churn reading
input:
  - deals.csv         # the cohorts: when each business entered
  - the F-1 concentration and main-businesses disclosures (~L401, ~L375)
computation:
  - share of current-period revenue generated by the businesses held at a past reference period
    (the 100% → 24% collapse, Q1 2024 → Q1 2026, ~L401)
  - who enters and who drops off the "main businesses" list per period (Meetup and Issuu, acquired
    2024, already off the Q1 2026 list, ~L375; Tractive excluded — closed after the period, ~L231)
output: the impermanence reading [derived] — the portfolio is a moving target; any inventory of the
  apps is stale on arrival. The durable objects are the Platform, the Playbook, and the
  capital-allocation discipline; the businesses flow through them.
```

This is why listing the apps as the model is a trap: any such inventory is stale on arrival, and it points attention at the outputs instead of the machine that produces them. In Q1 2026 the F-1's own "main businesses" list (alphabetical) was AOL, Brightcove, Eventbrite, Evernote, Harvest, komoot, Remini, StreamYard, Vimeo, and WeTransfer (`bsp-f1`, ~L375) — note that Meetup and Issuu, acquired in 2024, had already dropped off the "main" list, and Tractive (closed 18 May 2026) was excluded from all statistics as it fell after the reporting period (`bsp-f1`, ~L231). The durable objects are the Platform, the Playbook, and the Capital-allocation discipline; the businesses flow through them. The `deals.csv` list (in `../../world-model/company/`) is kept only as a cited output, with that caution written on it.

The impermanence is also the evidence that the moat is the machine and not any brand: if a single app were the asset, concentration would not collapse from 100% to 24% in two years while the company grew. It does, because the asset is the acquisition-and-transformation capability, not the app.
